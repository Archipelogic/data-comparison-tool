Understanding Correlation Matrices

What they show: Correlation matrices display the linear relationships between all pairs of numeric variables within each dataset. Each cell shows a correlation coefficient ranging from -1 to +1.

How to interpret the values:
  • Dark Red (close to -1): Strong negative correlation - as one variable increases, the other decreases
  • Light Yellow/White (close to 0): No linear relationship - variables are independent
  • Dark Blue (close to +1): Strong positive correlation - variables increase together
Why this matters for data comparison:
  • Consistency Check: Similar correlation patterns across datasets suggest consistent relationships between variables
  • Data Quality: Unexpected correlations may indicate data issues or different data generation processes
  • Feature Selection: Highly correlated variables (>0.9 or <-0.9) might be redundant
  • Model Building: Different correlation structures between datasets may require different modeling approaches
Common correlation strengths:
  • 0.7 to 1.0: Strong positive relationship
  • 0.3 to 0.7: Moderate positive relationship
  • -0.3 to 0.3: Weak or no relationship
  • -0.7 to -0.3: Moderate negative relationship
  • -1.0 to -0.7: Strong negative relationship
